We Dont Know What Orbits Our Planet

The need for a Space Debris Analytics Program

Human Space Age

Sputnik 1 launched the human space age in 1957. While human made objects in our orbit have consistently increased since then, commercial and expanded governmental interests have led demand to be at an all time high in recent years.
Height of the Space Race b/w US & Soviet
Union. 156 Rocket launches in 1967.
2018 (117 launches) was first year since 1990 with over 100 rocket launches.

So Whats the Problem????

Each rocket launch deploys one or many satellites that orbit the earth, these satellites remain in space for a very long time., creating Space Debris The more satellites orbiting the earth, the larger the probability they will collide with one another, causing major disruption to networks such as telecommunication, navigation, and other critical systems we rely on satellites for.
*** The primary concern is with LEO (Low- Earth Orbit) objects.
24k Currently tracked objects > 10cm

WE ARE NOT GOOD AT MEASURING SMALL SPACE OBJECTS

We currently have the ability to track only about 5% of objects 2 cm or greater in size.
The US Government and other select international agencies/ organizations catalog objects orbiting earth. However, the accuracy of these catalogs are limited to objects larger than 10cm . Estimations of the number of objects smaller than 10 cm agree that there are at least 12x more of them, however the estimations of how many more vary wildly.

Satellite BOOM

Operational Satellites have nearly doubled in the last 5 years. The invention of smaller, cheaper, more efficient satellites projects further exponential increase. Accounting for only commercial projections from major companies, operational satellites are expected to more than triple in the next 5 years. All of these projected satellitesare slated to go to LEO.


Kessler Syndrome

Visualization of Iridium/Cosmos collision 2009

Not acting will lead to our orbit being unusable- a condition known as the Kessler Syndrome. Cascading collisions will leave us with a dense cloud of space debris above us. The first ever example of this was in 2009 when a US Commercial Satellite Iridium crashed into a Russian defunct satellite Cosmos. This one collision caused thousands of additional Space Debris.
WE CAN PREVENT DOOMSDAY
This program will utilize modern tracking tools and analytic models to improve our ability to track orbiting objects. It will calibrate radar and telescope data from around the world to feed the models which will calculate the altitude and velocity of orbiting space debris. The increased precision of radar / telescope data and computational power of our analytic models will allow for small 1cm- 10cm objects to be finally tracked and cataloged.

It is critical for Humans to better understand what is orbiting our planet Without this, we risk prohibiting ourselves from having a usable orbit , which we will need to continue improving in technology, education, and further exploration into outer space.
